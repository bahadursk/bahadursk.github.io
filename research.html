Research

Mechanism-driven research in spectroscopy and functional organic photonics.

AURA Lab studies how excited-state behaviour at the molecular scale translates into application-grade performance — from triplet-state dynamics and through-bond interactions to afterglow imaging, OLED-relevant emitters, and donor–acceptor system design.

Programme

Six interconnected research themes

Organic Afterglow Materials

Designing molecular scaffolds and crystalline hosts that sustain long-lived organic emission. We work on through-bond interactions, host–guest crystal engineering, and donor–acceptor placement to control red, yellow, and high-resolution long-wavelength afterglow.

Excited-State Spectroscopy & Photophysics

Using steady-state and time-resolved spectroscopy to dissect singlet–triplet dynamics, Förster-resonance energy transfer, and intersystem crossing in multichromophoric and donor–acceptor architectures.

OLED & TADF Emitter Engineering

Designing thermally activated delayed fluorescence emitters with optimised external quantum efficiency, low efficiency roll-off, and tuned colour purity by manipulating donor linkages and acceptor nitrogen positions.

Multichromophoric & Functional Materials

Building rigid and flexible donor–acceptor systems for triplet harvesting, deep-blue/narrowband emission, and solid-state luminescence relevant to displays and sensing.

Persistent Phosphorescence & Anti-Counterfeiting

Mechanism-guided design of long-lived emission for irradiance-induced anti-counterfeit readout, condensed triplet accumulation, and information-storage workflows.

High-Resolution Afterglow Imaging

Exploiting persistent organic emission for high-contrast, low-background bioimaging using accessible CCD-based detection.

Methods & capabilities

A spectroscopy-led laboratory pipeline

  • Organic synthesis and emitter scaffold design
  • Steady-state UV–Vis absorption and emission
  • Time-resolved photoluminescence (ns to ms regime)
  • Cyclic voltammetry and electrochemical characterisation
  • Quantum-chemical calculations and computational screening
  • OLED-oriented materials evaluation and device-relevant testing
  • Crystal-host engineering for long-lived emission
  • Photophysical interpretation for imaging, sensing, and security workflows

Application pathways

Where the science meets devices and impact

  • Energy-efficient lighting and display technologies
  • High-resolution organic afterglow bioimaging
  • Persistent luminescence sensing platforms
  • Anti-counterfeiting and security materials
  • Optical information storage
  • Functional photonic devices and flexible electronics

Featured Direction

High-resolution organic afterglow imaging.

Organic afterglow compounds are promising candidates for high-resolution bioimaging because their persistent emission can reduce autofluorescence and background interference, enabling low-cost CCD-based imaging workflows. Our recent work has pushed afterglow into the long-wavelength regime through selective through-bond interactions and symmetry-breaking triplet excited states.
